Mihail Popov

Research Scientist at TADaaM team, Inria Bordeaux, France.

Short bio

I received the Ph.D. degree from the University of Versailles/Exascale Computing Research lab in 2016 under the supervision of Pablo de Oliveira Castro and William Jalby. Following a research visit to Intel Champaign in 2017, I was appointed postdoctoral researcher with David Black-Schaffer at the Uppsala Architecture Research Team in Sweden. In 2020, I transitioned to industry as a senior researcher at the Huawei-Edinburgh Joint Lab. In 2021, I joined Inria Bordeaux as a tenured research scientist (ISFP).

Research interest in performance and energy optimization with AI

The software stack presents an array of diverse levers and controls to align applications with the unique characteristics of the hardware they run on. During the compilation phase, one can select from a multitude of optimization passes, while at runtime, different interfaces enable fine-tuning of parallelism, thread placement, MPI parameters, I/O optimizations, data locality, and workload scheduling. Interestingly, hardware capabilities can, in turn, adapt to the specifics of the application. They include prefetching strategies, cache management, bandwidth allocation, and even core/uncore frequencies. Therefore, true efficiency necessitates navigating through a vast and ever-evolving space of both hardware and software optimizations.

Over the last couple of years, I have been working on the benefits and challenges of exploring such large optimization spaces. In particular, I consider how to combine machine learning with static compiler-based characterization methods to guide optimization at a lower cost. I also try to quantify the interconnectedness of these complex systems by highlighting the performance and energy impact from one optimization to others.

Current goals

Current hardware trends lead to more complex parallel applications with hardware heterogeneity, new scalable algorithms, and combinations of parallel programming models that pose many programmability challenges. Such complexity brings:

  1. an increase in the number of bugs requiring scalable debugging techniques to assist developers
  2. challenges in fully optimizing the different computation layers (e.g., compilers, runtimes, OS, hardware parameters) to achieve full efficiency

My two current goals are to harness machine-learning techniques for 1. assisted bug detection and correction, targeting hard domains such as parallel program verification with code embeddnigs and Large Language Models, and 2. fine-tuned cross-computation layers optimizations.
